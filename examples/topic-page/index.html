Topic introduction

A gas in a sealed container is made up of fast-moving particles that constantly bump into the walls. Each collision pushes outward — that push, spread over the area of the wall, is what we call pressure.

Three properties of the gas determine how strong that push is: how hot the gas is (temperature, T), how much room it has (volume, V), and how many particles are present (amount in moles, n). The ideal gas equation PV = nRT brings these together. R is a fixed number — the gas constant — that makes the units balance.

The ideal gas model assumes particles have negligible volume of their own and exert no attractive forces on each other — assumptions that hold well at low pressure and high temperature. PV = nRT did not arrive in one step. It is the combination of three empirical proportionalities established before the kinetic-molecular model was complete.

Boyle (1662) showed that P is inversely proportional to V at constant temperature and amount of gas. Charles (1787) showed V is directly proportional to T at constant P and n. Gay-Lussac (1809) showed P is directly proportional to T at constant V and n. Each was a separate empirical law. Together they imply that PV / T is a constant for any fixed amount of gas; Avogadro's law (V ∝ n at constant T and P) supplies the proportionality, completing PV = nRT.

The combined equation is exact for an ideal gas. Real gases, especially polar or larger-molecule species like CO₂, deviate at low temperature and high pressure where the model's two assumptions break down. Key concept

Pressure, volume, temperature, and amount of gas are linked through the equation PV = nRT.

What the IB syllabus says

Adapted from the IB Chemistry Guide 2025, Structure 1.5 (Ideal Gases).

  • S1.5.1 The kinetic-molecular theory describes an ideal gas as a collection of particles in constant random motion, with negligible volume, no intermolecular forces, and perfectly elastic collisions.
  • S1.5.2 Real gases deviate from ideal behaviour at low temperature and high pressure, where intermolecular forces and the volume of the particles themselves become significant.
  • S1.5.3 At standard temperature and pressure (273.15 K, 100 kPa), the molar volume of an ideal gas is 22.7 dm³ mol⁻¹.
  • S1.5.4 Boyle's law, Charles's law, Gay-Lussac's law, the combined gas law, and the ideal gas equation (PV = nRT) relate pressure, volume, temperature, and amount of gas. All temperatures must be in kelvin.

The ideal gas equation

P · V = n · · T

where P is pressure, V is volume, n is amount of gas in moles, T is absolute temperature in kelvin, and R is the molar gas constant. The four individual gas laws — Boyle, Charles, Gay-Lussac, and Avogadro — are special cases of this single relationship.

Worked example

Question: A 2.0 L container holds 0.5 mol of N₂ at 300 K. Calculate the pressure of the gas.

Show solution
  1. Identify the equation: PV = nRT, so P = nRT / V.
  2. Substitute (using R = 8.314 J·K⁻¹·mol⁻¹, V in m³):
    V = 2.0 L = 2.0 × 10⁻³ m³
    P = (0.5 × 8.314 × 300) / (2.0 × 10⁻³)
  3. Calculate: P = 6.24 × 10⁵ Pa ≈ 624 kPa.

Practice question

V = nRT / P = (0.25 × 8.314 × 250) / (150 × 10³) = 3.46 × 10⁻³ m³ ≈ 3.46 L

Common misconceptions

"Gas pressure is caused by gas particles pushing each other."

✗ Wrong: particles only exert measurable force when they collide with the container walls, not when passing each other.

✓ Right: pressure is the force per unit area from particle-wall collisions. Inter-particle collisions redistribute energy but don't directly cause container pressure.
